\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This report synthesizes roughly 12 hours of parallel computational work executed overnight across up to 8 concurrent Claude Code sessions. Four numbered phases of structured research (Phases 13--16) and seven topical sessions (A/C/D/E/F/H) advanced the spectral-phase Raman-suppression program on multiple fronts:
(i) a \emph{determinism fix} that restores bit-for-bit reproducibility of the L-BFGS optimizer at a 21.4\% runtime cost,
(ii) a \emph{Hessian eigenspectrum diagnosis} that proves the canonical optima are saddle points rather than minima,
(iii) a \emph{low-resolution phase parameterization} sweep showing $\mathrm{N}_\varphi \approx 128$ shaper pixels already reach the full-resolution suppression floor, and
(iv) a \emph{robustness / stability study} showing the simple-phase optimum is sharp (razor-thin basin, $\sigma_{3\text{dB}} = 0.025$ rad) but is an excellent warm-start initializer for L-BFGS at nearby operating points, reaching $-79.5$ dB on HNLF from an SMF-28 seed.
Parallel scaffolding for a multi-variable optimizer (Session A), multimode-fiber extension (Session C), 100-m long-fiber propagation (Session F), and a cost-function head-to-head audit (Session H) is code-complete but was blocked at execution time by a burst-VM freeze. Seven session branches remain unmerged to \texttt{main}. The report ends with a concrete inventory of open issues and recommended next actions.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Landed results}

\begin{keyfinding}{Physics results with quantitative support}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Phase 13 --- Landscape diagnosis.} At both canonical L-BFGS optima the Hessian is indefinite ($|\lambda_{\min}|/\lambda_{\max} = 2.6\%$ on SMF-28, $0.41\%$ on HNLF). \section{Phase 15 --- Deterministic Numerical Environment}
\label{sec:phase15}

\subsection{Motivation}

Phase 13 had uncovered a non-determinism failure: two runs with identical \texttt{Random.seed!(42)} and identical configuration produced $\varphi_{\mathrm{opt}}$ differing by $\max|\Delta\varphi| = 1.041$ rad and $\Delta J = -1.83$ dB. Root cause was traced to \texttt{FFTW.MEASURE}'s timing-based plan selection, amplified through L-BFGS into fully different optimization trajectories.

\subsection{What was done}

Eighteen call sites across \texttt{src/simulation/} were swapped from \texttt{FFTW.MEASURE} to \texttt{FFTW.ESTIMATE}. Six entry-point scripts now invoke a new \texttt{ensure\_deterministic\_environment()} helper which pins the planner and single-threads FFTW/BLAS.

\section{Phase 13 --- Optimization Landscape Diagnostics}
\label{sec:phase13}

Phase 13 asked: why do random starts and small $(L,P)$ perturbations yield visibly different $\varphi_{\mathrm{opt}}(\omega)$ profiles? Three orthogonal workstreams triangulated the answer.

\subsection{Workstream 1 --- Determinism}
\label{sec:phase13-w1}

\textbf{Verdict: FAIL} (addressed by Phase 15, \S\ref{sec:phase15}). Same seed, same config, two runs: $\max|\Delta\varphi| = 1.041$ rad, $\Delta J = -1.83$ dB ($J_a = -67.27$ dB vs $J_b = -65.45$ dB).

\begin{verdict}{Phase 13 headline verdict: \texttt{indefinite\_hessian}}
The ``different starts $\rightarrow$ different $\varphi_{\mathrm{opt}}$'' symptom is L-BFGS halting at \emph{different saddles} whose stable-manifold directions happen to intersect the random-start cone. The negative-curvature directions have magnitude 0.4--2.6\% of $\lambda_{\max}$ --- small, but numerically robust and not noise. This motivates the Phase 14 (sharpness-aware) and seed \texttt{newton-method-implementation.md} (Newton-on-reduced-subspace) follow-ups.
\end{verdict}

\textbf{Known limitation.} Arpack's matrix-free Lanczos wings cannot resolve $\lambda \approx 0$ without shift-invert (which is impossible for a matrix-free HVP). The two gauge null-modes predicted by symmetry are therefore a theorem but not a measured eigenvector in this phase.

\section{Phase 14 --- Sharpness-Aware Cost}
\label{sec:phase14}

\subsection{Goal}

Build a parallel path \texttt{optimize\_spectral\_phase\_sharp} that minimizes a regularized cost
\[
  J_{\mathrm{sharp}}(\varphi) = J(\varphi) + \lambda \cdot S(\varphi)
\]
where $S$ is a Hutchinson-style trace-of-Hessian estimator using gauge-projected Rademacher directions --- penalizing sharp minima in favor of flat basins, directly addressing the Phase 13 saddle problem. The vanilla path must remain byte-unchanged.

\subsection{Status}

\textbf{Plan 14.01 complete; Plan 14.02 (A/B sweep and robustness study) not executed.}

\subsubsection{14.01 deliverables}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \texttt{scripts/sharpness\_optimization.jl} --- 496 LoC with \texttt{build\_gauge\_projector}, \texttt{sharpness\_estimator}, \texttt{cost\_and\_gradient\_sharp}, \texttt{optimize\_spectral\_phase\_sharp}.
  \item \textbf{11 / 11 unit tests pass.} Taylor-remainder slope $= 1.947$ (target $\approx 2$) for $S(\varphi)$.
  \item \textbf{Vanilla snapshot locked} (SMF-28 canonical, seed $= 42$, 15 iter): $J_{\mathrm{linear}} = 1.21 \times 10^{-6}$, i.e.\ $-59.16$ dB. Stored in \texttt{results/raman/phase14/vanilla\_snapshot.jld2}.
  \item \textbf{FFTW wisdom exported and reimported} across processes: $\max|\Delta\varphi| = 1.5 \times 10^{-4}$ rad between fresh Julia subprocesses --- approximately 4 orders of magnitude better than Phase 13's baseline 1.04 rad. (Phase 15 subsequently achieved 0.0 with ESTIMATE planner.)
  \item Defaults: \texttt{SO\_DEFAULT\_LAMBDA $= 0.1$}, $N_s = 8$ Hutchinson samples, $\epsilon = 10^{-3}$ finite-difference step.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{14.02 pending}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item A/B matrix: 3 configs $\times$ 5 $\lambda$ values $\times$ 90 min burst allocation.
  \item Robustness study: 720 perturbation trials (the ``does sharpness escape saddles?'' acceptance test).
  \item No \texttt{ab\_results.jld2}, \texttt{robustness\_results.jld2}, or \texttt{FINDINGS.md} on disk.
\end{itemize}

\begin{blocker}{Outstanding risk}
Phase 13 cautioned that a pure $\mathrm{tr}(H)$ penalty can sign-cancel at saddle points (equal positive and negative curvature integrate to $\approx 0$) and recommended a signed-absolute variant $\sum |\lambda_i|$. The current implementation uses the straight Hutchinson trace. The 14.02 A/B sweep is the test that would detect this. Until it runs, we do not know whether sharpness-aware optimization actually escapes the Phase 13 saddles.
\end{blocker}

\section{Phase 16 / Session E --- Low-Resolution Phase Sweep}
\label{sec:sessionE}

\subsection{Motivation}

Real 4-$f$ pulse shapers have $\mathcal{O}(10^2)$ pixels, not $\mathrm{Nt} \approx 10^4$. This session asked: how few shaper knobs $\mathrm{N}_\varphi$ does the optimizer really need to reach the full-resolution suppression floor? The parameterization uses a fixed linear basis $B \in \mathbb{R}^{\mathrm{Nt} \times \mathrm{N}_\varphi}$, optimizes over $c \in \mathbb{R}^{\mathrm{N}_\varphi}$, and pulls the gradient back via $\partial J / \partial c = B^\top \, \partial J / \partial \varphi$.

\begin{keyfinding}{Surprising physics observation}
$\mathrm{N}_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 2.0$ across all $\mathrm{N}_\varphi$ levels. The optimized phase is \emph{effectively a 2-mode phase} (DC $+$ quadratic chirp) regardless of the optimization degree of freedom. This matches Phase 13's finding that optimal L-BFGS phases have low-order polynomial dominance plus gauge modes.

\textbf{Implication.} A 2-pixel shaper is already ``in the right ballpark.'' A 32-pixel shaper reaches $-58$ dB ($\sim 10$ dB shy of optimum) with no special tuning. A 128-pixel shaper saturates at the full-res floor.
\end{keyfinding}

Pulse bandwidth is only 157 bins (1.0\% of $\mathrm{Nt} = 16384$), so $\mathrm{N}_\varphi > 157$ is a physical impossibility; the full-res row is included only as a reference point and is identical to $\mathrm{N}_\varphi = 128$ within the numerical tolerance. Identity-basis self-test returns $|\Delta J| = 0.0$ and $\max|\Delta \mathrm{grad}| = 0.0$ versus the full-resolution path (byte-exact).

\section{Session D --- Simple-Phase Stability Study (Phase 17)}
\label{sec:sessionD}

\subsection{Question}

The Sweep-2 Pareto candidate at SMF-28, $L=0.25$ m, $P=0.1$ W reached $-82$ dB with a visually simple phase. Is that simplicity \emph{physically meaningful} (wide flat basin, transferable across configs) or \emph{coincidental} (razor-sharp, lucky)?

\subsection{Baseline}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $J_{\mathrm{final}} = -76.862$ dB in 25.02 s over 21 L-BFGS iterations.
  \item Nonlinear phase $\Phi_{\mathrm{NL}} = 1.63$ rad, $P_{\mathrm{peak}} = 2959.1$ W.
  \item Number of stationary points of $\varphi_{\mathrm{opt}}(\omega)$ across the pulse bandwidth: 7 (vs.\ 16 for the Phase 13 canonical $-68$ dB optimum).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Perturbation study --- is the basin flat?}

\subsection{Transferability --- warm-start is surprisingly robust}

Eval-only transfer (apply the baseline $\varphi_{\mathrm{opt}}$ unchanged to 7 other SMF-28 $(L,P)$ targets) keeps $J$ within 3 dB of the warm-start-reoptimized value in \textbf{0 / 7} cases --- catastrophic.

But L-BFGS \emph{warm-started} from the baseline $\varphi_{\mathrm{opt}}$ reaches $-70$ to $-82$ dB in 6--40 iterations on nearby configs, including \textbf{HNLF at $-79.5$ dB} --- a fiber the baseline was never computed for. Pearson correlation between stationary-point count and $J_{\mathrm{dB}}$ across the 4 tested optima: $r = 0.941$.

\begin{verdict}{Session D verdict}
\textbf{SHARP\_LUCKY (primary) $+$ SIMPLE\_PHASE\_IS\_GOOD\_WARM\_START\_INITIALIZER (secondary).}

The simple-phase profile lives in a \emph{stable manifold of many nearby basins}, so simplicity is a useful \emph{initialization prior} but not a \emph{generalization property} of one attractor. Low-$\Phi_{\mathrm{NL}}$ attractors are structurally simpler than high-$\Phi_{\mathrm{NL}}$ ones, but simplicity predicts neither basin width nor parameter transferability on its own.

\textbf{Experimental consequence.} SLM-class shaper calibration noise would destroy the $-77$ dB advantage. Before pushing any candidate to the lab, either (i) find a flatter basin, or (ii) validate closed-loop re-optimization with a phase-error estimator. The Sweep-E candidates at $\mathrm{N}_\varphi = 57$ should be re-run through Session D's protocol to see whether low-res parameterization buys robustness in exchange for the 2--10 dB it gives up on depth.
\end{verdict}

\section{Session A --- Multi-Variable Spectral Optimizer}
\label{sec:sessionA}

\subsection{Design}

The new \texttt{optimize\_spectral\_multivariable} path jointly optimizes any subset of
\[
  \{\varphi(\omega),\, A(\omega),\, E\}
\]
in a single forward/adjoint solve per L-BFGS iteration. Shaping formula:
\[
  u_{\mathrm{shaped}}(\omega, m) \;=\; \alpha \cdot A(\omega) \cdot e^{i\varphi(\omega)} \cdot c_m \cdot u_{\omega 0}(\omega, m)
\]
where $\alpha = \sqrt{E / E_{\mathrm{ref}}}$ normalizes pulse energy. A mode-coefficient variable $c_m$ is stubbed for later Session C extension.

\subsection{Engineering features}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Single adjoint $\lambda_0$ reprojected into per-variable gradient blocks (one backward solve covers all variables).
  \item Diagonal block preconditioning: $s_\varphi = 1$, $s_A = 1/\delta_{\mathrm{bound}}$, $s_E = 1/E_{\mathrm{ref}}$.
  \item \texttt{Fminbox(LBFGS(m=10))} for the amplitude-bound constraint $A(\omega) \in [1 - \delta, 1 + \delta]$.
  \item \texttt{log\_cost=true} default (optimize in dB, per the project's proven-good choice).
\end{itemize}
